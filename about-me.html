  • Work:

    I am a PhD candidate in physics at the California Institute of Technology (Caltech), and a graduate research assistant at the Laser Interferometer Gravitational-wave Observatory (LIGO). I am a member of the LIGO Scientific Collaboration. My thesis advisor is Prof. Alan J. Weinstein.

    I work on gravitational-wave (GW) signal detection and astrophysical/cosmological inference of GW signals with interferometric detectors. In particular, I lead the algorithmic development of an end-to-end data pipeline to map out the stochastic GW background intensity on the sky using various statistical methods. Also, along with my colleagues, I develop and operate an analysis pipeline to identify and characterize weak GW signals in noisy data through matched filtering.

    Besides physics and astronomy, I am enthusiastic about computational science and I am pursuing a Minor in Computational Science and Engineering at Caltech. I love to learn and apply state-of-the-art techniques not just to conduct research, but also to solve hard and interesting problems.

    Before Caltech, I obtained my Bachelor's degrees in Astronomy-Physics with High Distinctions and in Mathematics from the University of Virginia (UVA) in 2015. I was an Echols Scholar at UVA College of Arts & Sciences and I was inducted into Sigma Pi Sigma - American physics honor society - at UVA. The top 3 undergraduate research projects I was deeply involved in include searching for dark matter indirectly in the NOvA Collaboration at the Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory, improving GW searches at Caltech LIGO as a Summer Undergraduate Research Fellow, and measuring cosmic ray doses at commercial flight altitudes in the NASA-UVA JefferSat Cosmic Ray Mission. After UVA, I took a gap year and studied Higgs Boson trigger efficiency at the CMS Experiment at CERN in Switzerland.
